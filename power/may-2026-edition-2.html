The Grid in May: An Apology, a Net Billing Revolution, and the Promise That Is Still Being Waited On

By Dr. Sunny Okonkwo 26 May 2026 10 min read Forward Outlook · May 2026

May 2026 will be remembered for three things that tell very different stories about where the Nigerian electricity sector actually stands. A minister who apologised. A regulator that moved. And a grid that continued to underperform against the promises made at the start of the year. Here is what happened — and what it means.

The Apology That Needed to Be Said

In March 2026, Power Minister Adebayo Adelabu did something that Nigerian ministers rarely do. He apologised. Standing before cameras at a press conference in Abuja, he said: "I want to apologise to Nigerians, officially now, as the minister of power, for this temporary issue that is causing hardship, especially during this dry season when there is so much heat everywhere."

The apology acknowledged what the data had already been showing for weeks. Gas supply to thermal power stations had fallen to approximately 692 million standard cubic feet per day by late February — less than 43 percent of the daily requirement of 1,588 MMSCF. The consequence was direct and brutal: generation fell to 3,331 MW in April, barely enough to power a large city, let alone a nation of over 200 million people.

Adelabu gave a two-week timeline for improvement, citing commitments from gas suppliers and ongoing pipeline repairs — particularly at infrastructure operated by Seplat Energy. A special committee was constituted to monitor compliance by gas producers with domestic supply obligations. Whether that committee produces results or becomes another layer of accountability theatre remains the central question for June.

An apology is not a solution. But it is a signal — that the gap between promise and performance has become too large to talk around. The minister who promised no more grid collapses in January 2026 was the same minister apologising for collapsed supply in March. That distance is the story of the sector in the first half of this year.

NERC Moves on Two Fronts Simultaneously

While generation was struggling, the Nigerian Electricity Regulatory Commission was unusually active. Two regulatory interventions in May deserve serious attention from anyone tracking where the sector is genuinely moving.

Net Billing Regulations 2026

NERC commenced implementation of the Net Billing Regulations 2026 in the first week of May — a framework that formally recognises prosumers in Nigeria's electricity market for the first time. Under the new rules, eligible consumers can install solar photovoltaic systems, use the power themselves, and export surplus electricity to the grid under a net billing arrangement. The eligibility threshold is set between 50 kilowatt-peak and 1.5 megawatt-peak — meaning the policy initially benefits larger homes, commercial and industrial sites, and institutions rather than small residential users.

The financial upside for participants will be modest initially — energy credits offset future bills rather than generating direct income. But the regulatory signal is significant. Nigeria is formally moving toward a more decentralised, commercially oriented electricity system. Every solar panel on a commercial rooftop that feeds back to the grid is one less megawatt the national system needs to generate, transmit, and distribute.

Band A Compensation Directive

In a separate action, NERC approved a compensation package for Band A customers who received inadequate supply between February and March 2026 due to the gas shortage. Prepaid customers will receive token credits. Postpaid customers will receive bill adjustments. Distribution companies have been required to complete the compensation process by the end of June 2026.

This is notable for a reason that goes beyond the immediate financial relief. It establishes a regulatory precedent: that Band A service commitments are enforceable obligations, not aspirational targets. When the sector fails to meet those commitments — even for reasons outside the DisCos' direct control — there is now a compensation mechanism. That precedent will matter as the sector attempts to move more consumers into higher tariff bands with higher service expectations.

The Decentralisation Story Nobody Is Covering Properly

Beyond the headline generation numbers, a structural transformation is underway in Nigeria's electricity governance that will matter more in the long run than any single month's megawatt average. As at June 2026, twelve states — including Enugu, Ekiti, Lagos, Ondo, Oyo, Edo, Kogi, Plateau, Ogun, Ebonyi, Taraba, and Delta — have enacted their own state electricity acts under the framework of the Electricity Act 2023.

NERC has formally transferred regulatory oversight for intrastate electricity operations to fifteen states. The implications are significant and underappreciated. States are no longer passive recipients of a centralised national grid system. They are increasingly building their own sub-national electricity markets — with their own generation, their own regulation, and in some cases their own distribution arrangements. The Aba model — where Geometric Power's 188 MW plant has delivered consistent supply to roughly 900,000 people entirely independently of the national grid — is increasingly less an anomaly and more an early proof of concept for what the decentralised future looks like.

The Continental Context: Cape Town Is Watching

The Africa Energy Forum returns to Cape Town from 16 to 19 June 2026 — the continent's most significant annual gathering of energy investors, developers, and governments. The theme this year is Africa's move from energy access to industrial-scale infrastructure. The companies assembling there are deploying capital into transmission infrastructure, financing baseload capacity, and developing renewable projects at gigawatt scale.

The continental renewable energy picture has shifted dramatically. Africa added a record 11.3 gigawatts of renewable capacity in 2025 — triple the previous year — with South Africa, Egypt, and Ethiopia accounting for most of the growth. The investment is following the policy and the policy is following the economics: solar and storage are now the cheapest new electricity in most African markets.

Nigeria's representation at Cape Town will be watched. The country that carries the continent's largest economy and largest population should be leading this conversation. Whether the sector's current challenges — gas supply volatility, transmission constraints, metering gaps — are communicated as problems being actively solved, or as reasons for continued hesitation by international capital, will shape investor perception for the remainder of the year.

Forward Outlook: What June Must Deliver

Three things will determine whether May's mixed picture improves in June. First — and most urgent — whether the minister's two-week timeline for gas supply improvement actually materialized. The June Closing Analysis will carry the generation data that answers this question definitively. Second, whether DisCos begin processing the Band A compensation credits by the NERC deadline of end-June. Third, whether the TCN's ongoing grid upgrade work in Yola and Jalingo — expected to conclude by June 30 — delivers the transmission reliability improvements promised when the maintenance disruptions began.

The sector has spent the first five months of 2026 managing a gap between its January promises and its operational reality. June is the month that begins to close that gap — or confirms that it cannot be closed without structural interventions that no single month's policy activity can deliver.
