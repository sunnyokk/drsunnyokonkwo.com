May/June in Review: Governance Reform Advances, the Grid Stays Fragile, and Africa Gathers in Cape Town

By Dr. Sunny Okonkwo 1 Jun 2026 10 min read Closing Analysis · May–June 2026

The closing analysis for this period covers a sector doing two things simultaneously — advancing its structural reform architecture at a pace that genuinely impresses sector analysts, while delivering electricity performance that continues to fall well short of what the economy and the population require. Both things are true. Holding both is the only honest way to read this moment.

The Reform That Is Actually Happening

A Leadership newspaper analysis published on 1 June 2026 captured what is genuinely significant about where Nigeria's electricity governance stands. NERC's reforms under the Electricity Act 2023 represent governance innovation that is reshaping a once-centralised power system. Market liberalisation has introduced competitive generation, independent transmission operations, and performance-based distribution regulation. These are not cosmetic changes. They are the structural rewiring of how electricity is governed in Africa's largest economy.

The decentralisation trend is accelerating. As of June 2026, twelve states have enacted their own electricity legislation. NERC has transferred regulatory oversight to fifteen states for intrastate operations. The Presidential Metering Initiative has mobilised N700 billion through FAAC, with an additional $500 million World Bank facility in procurement for nationwide meter rollout. The Mini-Grid Regulations 2026 have established a clearer legal framework for off-grid and distributed energy development — the sector where much of Nigeria's electricity access growth will actually happen in the next decade.

The Performance Gap That Will Not Close Itself

Against the reform progress, the generation data tells its own story. Nigeria's installed generation capacity exceeds 13,000 MW. Peak delivery to the grid in 2025 rarely exceeded 5,500 MW, with daily averages closer to 4,000 MW. As of June 2026 the picture has not substantively improved. The gap between installed capacity and delivered power remains the sector's defining and most expensive failure.

The causes are well documented and consistently present: gas supply constraints that leave thermal plants running at a fraction of their rated output; transmission bottlenecks that prevent dispatched generation from reaching distribution networks; distribution infrastructure that cannot absorb higher volumes even when generation and transmission allow. NERC's own data shows transmission losses at 7.24 percent in 2025 — above the approved 7 percent benchmark — and the regulator has now set a 6.5 percent target for December 2026 with smart metering at all regional interconnection points required by the same date.

The sector spent the first six months of 2026 with its reformist hat and its operational failure in the same room, hoping observers would focus on the former. The honest analysis requires attention to both. Reform without performance improvement is a process, not a result.

TCN's Yola-Jalingo Upgrade: What It Means

TCN confirmed that its ongoing OPGW installation and maintenance work in the Yola and Jalingo corridor — which has been causing planned disruptions since earlier in the year — is expected to complete by June 30, 2026. The work involves optical fibre ground wire installation and related maintenance designed to improve grid monitoring, enhance operational efficiency, and strengthen transmission reliability across the affected areas.

This is the kind of unglamorous but essential infrastructure work that determines whether the grid gets more reliable over time. Improved grid monitoring means earlier detection of fault precursors. Better operational efficiency means fewer unnecessary curtailments. These are not headline-generating improvements — but they compound into the grid stability gains that eventually translate into consumer experience.

The Africa Energy Forum: What Cape Town Is Saying to Lagos

The Africa Energy Forum opens in Cape Town on 16 June 2026 with a theme that should be read carefully by Nigerian policy makers: Africa's move from energy access to industrial-scale infrastructure. The companies gathering there — ACWA Power, Infinity Power, AMEA Power, Globeleq among them — are developing gigawatt-scale renewable energy projects across the continent. They are deploying capital into transmission infrastructure. They are financing baseload capacity for heavy industry.

The continental renewable energy transition is accelerating in ways that were not fully anticipated even three years ago. Africa added 11.3 gigawatts of renewable capacity in 2025 — triple the previous year's addition. Solar and storage costs have fallen to the point where new renewable capacity is cheaper than new gas-fired generation in most African markets. The investment is following the economics.

Nigeria's position in this picture is complicated. The country has abundant renewable potential — solar irradiation levels that make it one of the world's most attractive large-scale solar markets. It also has a gas resource base that represents an enormous economic asset and a continuing role in power generation during the transition. Managing these two realities simultaneously — extracting value from gas while building toward a diversified, lower-carbon grid — is the energy strategy challenge that no administration has yet coherently addressed.

The Aba Model: Still the Most Important Story Not Being Told

When the national grid collapsed on January 23, 2026 — reducing generation to zero megawatts and cutting supply to all eleven DisCos — one city kept its lights on. Aba, in Abia State, continued to receive uninterrupted electricity through Geometric Power's 188 MW plant and its smart-metered distribution network.

The contrast was stark enough that Business Day noted it directly: while the rest of the country went dark, Aba's roughly 900,000 residents experienced no disruption. Analysts and business groups have since intensified calls for the Aba model to be replicated. The model works for reasons that are not mysterious: a private operator with full responsibility for generation and distribution, a commercially structured tariff, smart metering from the start, and no dependence on the national grid's chronic instabilities.

The Aba model is not a silver bullet for national electrification. But it is a proof of concept for what reliable electricity looks like in a Nigerian urban context when the incentive structures are correctly aligned. That proof of concept deserves to be at the centre of the sector's policy conversation in the second half of 2026.

What the Second Half of 2026 Must Deliver

The sector enters H2 2026 with its reform credentials stronger than at any recent point — the regulatory framework, the decentralisation architecture, and the investment attraction numbers all represent genuine progress. What remains absent is a convincing answer to the most basic question any electricity consumer asks: when will the power be on, reliably, for long enough to matter?

The minister's 6,000 MW target by year-end remains the stated ambition. Reaching it from a current average of approximately 4,000 MW requires sustained gas supply improvement, transmission capacity that can absorb higher dispatch volumes, and distribution infrastructure that can actually deliver the power to consumers. None of these conditions is currently met. All of them are theoretically improvable. Whether they improve in the next six months is the story this publication will track — edition by edition, with the data that is actually there, not the data the sector wishes were there.
